Summer is the grapevine's most active growth phase. After flowering and fruit set, the shoots continue to
grow, the leaves work at full capacity, and the young berries develop rapidly. This is when it becomes
clear how well the vine can keep vegetative growth and crop development in balance.
During photosynthesis, green leaves use light to produce sugars from water and carbon dioxide. This
sugar is not only the basis of the future wine&#39;s alcohol level, but also the plant&#39;s own energy source. The
vine directs this energy to where growth is strongest at that moment: the shoot tips, young leaves, lateral
shoots and developing berries.
For this reason, summer canopy work is not merely a matter of shaping the vine. When we position
shoots, manage lateral shoots or top the shoots, we are actually influencing the vine&#39;s light conditions,
aeration and energy distribution.



### The canopy: the vine's working surface

The canopy is the green surface of the vine row: shoots, leaves, tendrils and clusters together. The fruit
zone is the part of the canopy where the clusters are located. A good canopy is neither too dense nor too
bare: it carries enough leaves for photosynthesis while allowing light and air to move around the clusters.
Because the grapevine is naturally a climbing plant, it needs a trellis system. The trellis and orderly shoot
positioning help keep the canopy from collapsing, prevent the fruit zone from closing up, and allow
vineyard operations to remain possible between the rows.
An overly dense canopy has a measurable effect. According to a viticultural summary from Oregon State
University, the outer leaves of the canopy absorb or reflect more than 94% of the sunlight that reaches
them, leaving less than 6% for the inner leaves. Strongly shaded leaves therefore become less efficient:
they may use more energy than they produce.
Air movement is just as important. A closed, humid canopy dries more slowly after rain or dew and can
create a more favorable environment for fungal diseases. A well-organized, open canopy dries faster,
and plant-protection treatments can reach the clusters more evenly.

### Lateral shoots: not an enemy, but leaf area to be regulated

A lateral shoot develops from the leaf axil of the main shoot. It is not a fault, and it is not a plant part that
should automatically be removed. When young, it uses energy; later, however, its own leaves
photosynthesize, meaning that it can contribute to the vine&#39;s sugar production.
The question is not whether every lateral shoot should be removed, but where and how many should
remain. In the fruit zone, too many lateral shoots can make the canopy too dense: they shade the
clusters, reduce air movement, slow drying and make plant protection more difficult. Excessive removal,
on the other hand, can reduce the active leaf area and expose berries to overly strong sunlight or heat
load.
The effect of lateral shoots on must composition is not simple either. In a six-year Swiss study published
in 2024, researchers compared different ways of removing main leaves and lateral shoots in the fruit
zone. When the main leaves were removed but lateral shoots were allowed to develop, leaf area was
15% greater, yield potential was 12% lower, and the must contained 12% more malic acid, 10% more
yeast-assimilable nitrogen and 8% more glutathione compared with the treatment in which only the lateral
shoots were removed.

This does not mean that lateral shoots increase acidity in every situation. The exact effect depends on
the variety, the vintage, canopy density and the temperature of the fruit zone. The key point is rather that
main leaves and lateral shoots cannot simply be treated as interchangeable: they have different
physiological roles, and the way they are managed can also affect berry composition. Malic acid is
especially sensitive to temperature, so shading the fruit zone or opening it up too much can also
influence the perception of acidity in the later wine.

### Shoot topping: guiding growth rather than stopping it

Shoot topping is the cutting back of the upper part of overgrown shoots. It becomes necessary when
shoots outgrow the trellis, bend back, become tangled, shade the fruit zone or obstruct work between the
rows.
Biologically, shoot topping is far more than simply shortening a shoot. The shoot tip is one of the plant&#39;s
most active growing points. It is also the source of hormonal signals that help maintain the dominance of
the main shoot and suppress the growth of axillary buds. This is called apical dominance. One important
player is the plant hormone auxin, although sugar supply and other hormones are also involved in the
process.
When the shoot tip is removed, this inhibitory effect is reduced. The plant does not stop; it reorganizes its
growth. Longitudinal shoot growth slows, while new lateral shoots can more readily start to grow from the
axillary buds. This is why the canopy often begins to thicken again shortly after topping.
Timing is therefore crucial. Early topping - around flowering or immediately afterward - can trigger strong
lateral-shoot formation. Removing the actively growing shoot tip can temporarily leave more energy for
the inflorescences or young clusters, which may improve fruit set in certain varieties. The cost can be
more compact clusters and a canopy that later becomes dense again.
Later topping generally produces a more moderate growth response because the main flush of shoot
growth is already weakening. In a Luxembourg trial on Riesling and Pinot gris, researchers examined the
timing of the first shoot topping. When the first topping was carried out four weeks after the end of
flowering, clusters became looser, the point at which Botrytis bunch rot reached 5% disease severity was
delayed by up to 11 days, and total soluble solids were 0.77-2.24 Brix higher than in the standard
treatment, without a significant reduction in yield.
This, however, is not a universal rule that topping should always be done as late as possible. Waiting too
long can allow shoots to bend back, the fruit zone to close up, aeration to decline and plant protection to
become more difficult. Good timing always depends on the vigor of the variety, the season, vine water
supply and the current condition of the canopy.

### Why does all this matter for the wine?

Summer canopy operations shape wine quality not directly, but through the physiological condition of the
grapevine. They affect light exposure, fruit-zone temperature, air movement, disease risk and must
composition.
The goal is neither a completely bare fruit zone nor a closed, dense canopy. The goal is balance: enough
leaves for photosynthesis, enough light for ripening, enough air for drying, and enough shade to keep the
berries from excessive heat stress.
Good canopy work is therefore not a single movement, but a continuous series of decisions. The
grapevine reacts quickly, and the winegrower&#39;s task is not to stop this growth, but to guide it in the right
direction.
